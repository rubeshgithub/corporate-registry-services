Every corporation incorporated in Canada must file an annual return with its corporate registry every year. Missing this filing — even once — can result in administrative dissolution, late fees, and significant costs to revive the corporation. This guide covers what an annual return is, why it matters, and the specific deadline for every Canadian jurisdiction.

## What Is a Corporate Annual Return?

An annual return (also called an annual report or annual filing in some provinces) is a statutory filing that confirms your corporation still exists, is actively carrying on business, and has up-to-date information on record with the corporate registry.

It is **not** the same as a tax return filed with the Canada Revenue Agency. An annual return is filed with the provincial or federal corporate registry — not with the CRA — and contains corporate information (directors, registered office address, share structure) rather than financial information.

## Why the Annual Return Is Critical

Failure to file triggers a cascade of consequences:

1. **Notice of intent to dissolve** — the registry issues a formal warning
2. **Administrative dissolution** — the corporation is struck from the registry if it fails to respond
3. **Loss of corporate name** — once dissolved, your corporation's name is available for others to use
4. **Loss of liability protection** — a dissolved corporation may not protect its owners from personal liability
5. **Revival required** — reinstating a dissolved corporation requires a revival application, additional fees, and can cost several hundred to several thousand dollars
6. **Bank and financing issues** — banks and lenders check corporate status; a dissolved corporation cannot borrow in its name

## What Information Is Required in an Annual Return?

While requirements vary by jurisdiction, a standard annual return typically includes:

- Full legal name of the corporation
- Corporate registry number
- Registered and records office address
- Names and addresses of all current directors and officers
- Principal business activities
- Date of the most recent annual general meeting (or resolution in lieu)
- Share structure confirmation (in some provinces)

Some provinces (notably British Columbia) require additional disclosure of share capital and authorized signing officers.

## What Is the "Anniversary Date"?

Many provinces tie the annual return deadline to the corporation's **anniversary date** — the date the corporation was originally incorporated. For example, if your Ontario corporation was incorporated on March 15, its anniversary date is March 15 every year.

Federal corporations use the anniversary **month** (not the specific date), and the annual return is due within 60 days of that month's end.

For Québec corporations and Manitoba corporations, the deadline is a fixed calendar date (March 31) regardless of when the corporation was incorporated.

## Can You File a Late Annual Return?

In most jurisdictions, yes — but there are consequences. Some provinces charge late filing fees. If too much time passes, the registry will issue a dissolution notice. At that point, you can still file a revival application, but the cost and effort increase significantly.

There is no maximum "catch-up" period in most jurisdictions — even a corporation that has missed three or four annual returns can often be revived, but the process becomes more complex and expensive with each passing year.

## Extra-Provincial Annual Returns

If your corporation is registered as an extra-provincial corporation in one or more provinces (in addition to your home jurisdiction), you must also file annual returns in each of those provinces. These are separate filings from your home-jurisdiction annual return and have their own deadlines and fees.
