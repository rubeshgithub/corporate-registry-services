One of the first decisions every Canadian entrepreneur faces when incorporating is whether to incorporate federally under the *Canada Business Corporations Act* (CBCA) or provincially under a provincial business corporations act. Both options create a valid, limited-liability corporation — but they differ significantly in scope, cost, name protection, and compliance obligations.

This guide walks through every key difference so you can make an informed decision.

## The Core Difference: Where Your Corporation "Lives"

A **federally incorporated corporation** is created under federal law and is technically authorized to conduct business in every province and territory in Canada without separately registering as an extra-provincial corporation (in most provinces).

A **provincially incorporated corporation** is created under the law of one specific province and is only automatically authorized to carry on business within that province. To operate in other provinces, it must register as an extra-provincial corporation in each additional jurisdiction.

## Federal Incorporation: When It Makes Sense

Federal incorporation is the right choice if you:

- **Plan to operate across multiple provinces** — A federal corporation can carry on business nationally with minimal extra registration. A provincial corporation would need extra-provincial registration in each new province it enters.
- **Want coast-to-coast name protection** — A federal NUANS search reserves your corporate name across all of Canada. Provincial name protection only covers that province.
- **Are raising capital nationally or internationally** — Many investors and institutional lenders prefer or require federally incorporated entities for cross-jurisdictional transactions.
- **Operate in a regulated industry** — Some federal licences and contracts require a federally incorporated entity.
- **Plan to list on a stock exchange** — Publicly traded companies in Canada are almost always federally incorporated.

**Important caveat:** Even with federal incorporation, you must still register as an extra-provincial corporation in any province where you maintain a physical office, employees, or significant operations. The filing requirements vary by province. CRS can handle all extra-provincial registrations.

## Provincial Incorporation: When It Makes Sense

Provincial incorporation is the right choice if you:

- **Operate exclusively within one province** — If your business, employees, office, and customers are all in Ontario (or BC, Alberta, etc.), there is no practical reason to incorporate federally.
- **Want simpler ongoing compliance** — One annual return to one registry, rather than a federal return plus provincial extra-provincial filings.
- **Are in British Columbia, Alberta, or Québec** — These provinces have particularly efficient and business-friendly incorporation processes. BC and Alberta impose no Canadian residency requirement on directors, making them popular for international entrepreneurs.
- **Want straightforward startup costs** — CRS provincial incorporation is a flat $699 + GST/HST all-in, the same as federal.

## Director Residency Requirements

This is often the deciding factor for non-Canadian founders:

- **Federal (CBCA):** At least 25% of directors must be Canadian residents. For a corporation with a single director, that one director must be Canadian resident.
- **Ontario (OBCA):** At least 25% of directors must be Canadian residents.
- **British Columbia (BCA):** No Canadian residency requirement for directors.
- **Alberta (ABCA):** No Canadian residency requirement for directors.
- **Québec:** No Canadian residency requirement for directors.

If your founding team has no Canadian residents, incorporating in BC or Alberta may be more practical than incorporating federally.

## Annual Compliance Comparison

**Federal corporation:**
1. Annual Return to Corporations Canada (due within 60 days of the corporation's anniversary month) — includes list of directors and registered office address
2. Extra-provincial registration maintained in each province where the corporation carries on business

**Provincial corporation:**
1. Annual Return to the provincial registry (deadline varies by province — see our Annual Return Guide for province-by-province deadlines)
2. Extra-provincial registration in each additional province where business is conducted

## Numbered vs. Named Corporation

This decision applies to both federal and provincial incorporation. A **named corporation** (e.g., "Acme Consulting Inc.") requires a NUANS name search to confirm availability. A **numbered corporation** (e.g., "1234567 Ontario Inc.") gets automatically assigned the next available number with no name search required and can be incorporated faster.

Many businesses start with a numbered corporation and later file Articles of Amendment to add a name — a common and cost-effective approach.
