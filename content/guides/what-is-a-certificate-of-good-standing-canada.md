A Certificate of Good Standing is an official government-issued document that confirms a corporation is legally registered, currently active, and compliant with all statutory filing requirements in its jurisdiction. In Canada, this document goes by different names depending on the province — it may be called a Certificate of Status, Certificate of Compliance, or Certificate of Existence — but it serves the same purpose: it proves your company is in good legal standing with the corporate registry.

## What Does "Good Standing" Actually Mean?

A corporation in good standing has:

- Filed all required annual returns or reports on time
- Paid all outstanding fees to the corporate registry
- Not been dissolved, discontinued, struck off, or amalgamated into another company
- Maintained a valid registered office address on record

If any of these conditions are not met, the registry can place the corporation in "bad standing" or initiate administrative dissolution proceedings.

## Who Needs a Certificate of Good Standing?

Certificates of Good Standing are required in a wide range of business situations:

- **Bank financing and business loans** — lenders require proof of good standing before approving credit facilities or mortgages
- **Extra-provincial registration** — registering your corporation in a new province typically requires a Certificate of Good Standing from your home jurisdiction
- **Mergers and acquisitions** — legal due diligence in any M&A transaction includes confirming good standing in all jurisdictions
- **Government contracts and tenders** — federal and provincial governments often require vendors to demonstrate corporate good standing
- **Professional licensing** — many regulated professions and industries require certificate of good standing to apply for or renew licences
- **Foreign business expansion** — operating in the United States or internationally often requires a Canadian Certificate of Good Standing
- **Real estate transactions** — buying or selling property through a corporation typically triggers a registry check
- **Investor due diligence** — venture capital firms, angel investors, and private equity require good standing confirmation before closing any investment

## What Information Is on the Certificate?

A standard Canadian Certificate of Good Standing includes:

- Full legal name of the corporation
- Corporate registry number or business number
- Date of incorporation or registration
- Current legal status (active and in good standing)
- The jurisdiction that issued the certificate
- The signature or seal of the registrar
- The date the certificate was issued

Some jurisdictions also include the date of the most recent annual return filing and confirmation that no dissolution proceedings are pending.

## How Long Is It Valid?

Certificates of Good Standing are a point-in-time document. They confirm good standing as of the date of issue, not indefinitely. Most banks, law firms, and government bodies accept certificates that are no more than 30 to 90 days old. For major transactions, request a fresh certificate close to the closing date.
