Every corporation incorporated in Canada — federal or provincial — is legally required to maintain a corporate minute book. This is not optional. It is a statutory obligation under the *Canada Business Corporations Act*, the *Ontario Business Corporations Act*, the BC *Business Corporations Act*, and the equivalent legislation in every other Canadian jurisdiction.

Despite this requirement, minute books are one of the most commonly overlooked compliance obligations for small and medium-sized businesses.

## What Is a Corporate Minute Book?

A corporate minute book (also called a corporate records book) is the official record-keeping file for a corporation. It contains every critical legal document from the corporation's inception to the present day — organized and maintained to satisfy statutory requirements and provide a complete legal history of the corporation.

Think of it as the permanent legal file for your corporation: the original Articles of Incorporation are in there, every share certificate that has ever been issued, every director and officer appointment, every shareholders' resolution, and every annual meeting minute.

## What's Inside a Complete Minute Book?

A properly maintained corporate minute book contains:

**Constating Documents**
- Certificate of Incorporation (or Articles of Incorporation)
- By-Laws of the Corporation
- Unanimous Shareholders' Agreement (if applicable)

**Registers and Ledgers**
- Register of Directors (current and historical)
- Register of Officers
- Register of Shareholders (also called the Share Ledger)
- Share Transfer Register (record of every share transfer)

**Share Documents**
- Share Subscription agreements
- Share Certificates (one per issuance)
- Directors' Resolutions approving share allotments
- Consent and Waiver for allotment of shares

**Minutes and Resolutions**
- Minutes of the Organizational Meeting of Directors
- Minutes of the First Meeting of Shareholders
- Directors' Resolutions (passed throughout the corporation's life)
- Shareholders' Resolutions (passed at annual and special meetings)
- Annual General Meeting (AGM) minutes for each year

**Compliance Documents**
- Annual Returns (copies filed with the registry)
- Notices of Change filed with the registry
- CRA Business Number registration confirmation

## Is a Minute Book Legally Required in Canada?

Yes. Here is the specific language from key statutes:

**Canada Business Corporations Act (CBCA), Section 20:** Every corporation shall prepare and maintain records including a copy of the articles and any amendments, the by-laws, a register of directors, and a shareholders register.

**Ontario Business Corporations Act (OBCA), Section 140:** A corporation shall prepare and maintain, at its registered office or at some other place in Ontario designated by the directors, records containing the articles and any amendments to the articles; the by-laws and any amendments; a register of directors; a register of officers; and a securities register.

Similar provisions exist in the *Business Corporations Act* (BC), the *Business Corporations Act* (Alberta), and the equivalent statutes in every other Canadian province and territory.

## What Happens If You Don't Maintain a Minute Book?

The consequences of missing or disorganized corporate records can be severe:

- **Sale of the business** — buyers' lawyers conduct due diligence and require the minute book. An incomplete minute book can kill a deal or force a significant price reduction.
- **Bank financing** — lenders often request the minute book (or a certified copy) before approving a business loan or line of credit.
- **Shareholder disputes** — without written records of share issuances, transfers, and board decisions, ownership disputes become very difficult (and expensive) to resolve.
- **CRA audit** — the Canada Revenue Agency may request corporate records during an audit. An incomplete minute book raises red flags about the legitimacy of corporate decisions.
- **Director liability** — directors who fail to maintain proper records can be personally liable in certain circumstances.
- **Fines** — under some provincial statutes, failure to maintain required corporate records can result in fines against the corporation and its directors.

## Who Is Responsible for Maintaining the Minute Book?

Under most provincial statutes, the responsibility for maintaining corporate records falls on the corporation's directors. In practice, this is typically handled by:

- The corporation's corporate lawyer (many law firms hold minute books in trust for their clients)
- The corporation's accountant or bookkeeper
- The business owner directly (common for small corporations)
- A corporate services provider like CRS

## How Often Does It Need to Be Updated?

The minute book is a living document. It must be updated every time a significant corporate event occurs, including:

- Electing or resigning directors or officers
- Issuing new shares or transferring existing shares
- Changing the registered office address
- Passing any director or shareholder resolution
- Filing an annual return
- Amending the by-laws or articles
- Entering into any major agreement requiring director approval

The annual general meeting (or an annual resolution in lieu of a meeting) generates new minutes that must be added to the minute book every year.

## Digital vs. Paper Minute Books

Historically, minute books were physical binders kept at the corporate lawyer's office or in a filing cabinet at the business. Today, most jurisdictions permit electronic minute books — digital files maintained securely and accessible remotely.

A digital minute book offers several advantages:
- Instantly accessible from anywhere
- Easy to share with lawyers, accountants, or buyers during due diligence
- No risk of loss due to fire, flood, or physical damage
- Significantly lower storage and management costs
